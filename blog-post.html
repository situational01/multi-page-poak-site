From Street Vendor to Business Owner: Mary's Journey

February 15, 2026 by Grace A. 5 min read

Mary Wanjiku, 42, used to wake up at 4 AM every day to sell vegetables on the streets of Kibera. Today, she owns a tailoring shop employing two other women and can afford to send all three of her children to school. This is her story.

Before POAK: A Daily Struggle

For 15 years, Mary worked as a street vendor in Nairobi's Kibera informal settlement. She would buy vegetables wholesale, carry them on her back, and walk for hours to find customers. On a good day, she earned 300 KES. On bad days, she went home with nothing.

Joining a VSLA Group

In 2023, Mary joined a POAK Village Savings and Loan Association (VSLA) in her community. The group of 25 women meets weekly, each contributing 100 KES to a shared fund. Members can take small loans from the group and repay with interest, building both savings and credit history.

The day I took my first loan of 5,000 KES, I cried. It was the first time anyone believed in me enough to lend me money.

- Mary Wanjiku

The First Loan: A Sewing Machine

After six months of saving, Mary took her first loan of 5,000 KES to buy a second-hand sewing machine. She had learned to sew from her mother and saw an opportunity.

Growing the Business

With subsequent loans, Mary bought more fabric and a second machine. Today, she rents a small shop in Kibera, employs two women from her VSLA group, and makes custom clothing for weddings and events.

20,000 KES Monthly Income
3 Children in School
2 Women Employed

Paying It Forward

Mary is now a mentor in the VSLA group, helping new members navigate their own journeys. She also teaches sewing to other women in the community.
